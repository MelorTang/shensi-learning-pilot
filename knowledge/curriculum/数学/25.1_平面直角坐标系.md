# 平面直角坐标系

## Core Idea
The core idea is to bridge the gap between algebra and geometry by introducing a coordinate system that assigns a unique pair of real numbers (coordinates) to every point in a plane. This enables the algebraic representation of geometric shapes, transformations, and relationships, forming the foundation of analytical geometry.

## Learning Steps
1. **Introduction to Coordinates**: Understand the Cartesian coordinate system, the x-axis, y-axis, origin, and the four quadrants.
2. **Plotting Points**: Learn how to represent a point with an ordered pair $(x, y)$ and how to plot points based on their coordinates.
3. **Coordinates of Objects**: Represent simple geometric figures and real-world positions using coordinates.
4. **Distance Formula**: Calculate the distance between two points $(x_1, y_1)$ and $(x_2, y_2)$ using the formula $\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$.
5. **Transformations**: Understand how geometric transformations affect coordinates:
   - **Translation (平移)**: Adding/subtracting constants to x or y.
   - **Axial Symmetry (轴对称)**: Reflecting across the x-axis $(x, -y)$ or y-axis $(-x, y)$.
   - **Homothety / Scaling (位似)**: Multiplying coordinates by a scale factor.

## Common Pitfalls
- **Order of Coordinates**: Swapping the x and y coordinates (e.g., plotting $(3, -2)$ as $(-2, 3)$).
- **Signs in Quadrants**: Forgetting the correct signs for points in the II, III, and IV quadrants.
- **Distance to Axes**: Confusing the distance to the x-axis (which is $|y|$) with the distance to the y-axis (which is $|x|$).
- **Transformation Rules**: Memorizing transformation rules incorrectly instead of visualizing them, leading to errors in reflections and translations.

## Parent Guidance
- **Real-World Connections**: Play games like Battleship or use GPS maps to show how coordinate systems are used in everyday life.
- **Visual Verification**: Encourage your child to always draw a rough sketch of the coordinate plane to verify if their calculated coordinates or distances make logical sense.
- **Hands-On Transformations**: Use graph paper to physically move, flip, and scale shapes, and have your child track how the coordinates of the vertices change.

## Linked Mistake Concepts
- **Absolute Value**: Mistakes in calculating distances often stem from a weak grasp of absolute values.
- **Pythagorean Theorem**: The distance formula is derived directly from the Pythagorean theorem; failure to understand the geometric connection can make the formula seem like an arbitrary memorization task.
- **Integers and Rational Numbers**: Errors in adding or subtracting negative coordinates during translations.

## Examples
**Example Problem:**
A triangle has vertices A(1, 2), B(4, 2), and C(1, 6). 
1. Find the lengths of sides AB, AC, and BC.
2. If the triangle is translated 3 units to the left and 2 units down, what are the coordinates of the new vertices A', B', and C'?

**Solution:**
```text
1. 
Points A(1, 2) and B(4, 2) lie on the same horizontal line (y-coordinates are equal). 
Length of AB = |4 - 1| = 3.
Points A(1, 2) and C(1, 6) lie on the same vertical line (x-coordinates are equal). 
Length of AC = |6 - 2| = 4.
To find BC, use the distance formula between (4, 2) and (1, 6):
BC = sqrt((1 - 4)^2 + (6 - 2)^2) = sqrt((-3)^2 + 4^2) = sqrt(9 + 16) = sqrt(25) = 5.
(Note: Triangle ABC is a right-angled triangle with legs 3 and 4, hypotenuse 5).

2. 
Translation rule: (x, y) -> (x - 3, y - 2).
A' = (1 - 3, 2 - 2) = (-2, 0)
B' = (4 - 3, 2 - 2) = (1, 0)
C' = (1 - 3, 6 - 2) = (-2, 4)
```
