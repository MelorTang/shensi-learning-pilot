# 反比例函数

## Core Idea
The core idea is to understand the mathematical relationship where two variables are inversely proportional, meaning their product is a non-zero constant ($xy = k$). This chapter explores the algebraic form $y = \frac{k}{x}$ and its corresponding geometric representation, the hyperbola. It highlights the concept of asymptotic behavior, where variables approach infinity or zero but never reach the axes.

## Learning Steps
1. **Concept of Inverse Proportion**: Understand the definition of an inverse proportional function $y = \frac{k}{x}$ (where $k$ is a constant and $k \neq 0$). 
2. **Graphing (Hyperbola)**: Learn how to plot the graph of $y = \frac{k}{x}$, recognizing that it consists of two disconnected branches.
3. **Properties**: Analyze how the sign of $k$ determines the quadrants the graph occupies (I and III if $k>0$; II and IV if $k<0$) and the monotonic behavior (increasing or decreasing) within each branch.
4. **Geometric Significance of $k$**: Understand that for any point $(x, y)$ on the graph, the area of the rectangle formed by the point and the axes is always $|k|$.
5. **Applications**: Solve real-world problems involving inverse proportionality, such as the relationship between speed and time for a fixed distance, or pressure and volume for a fixed mass of gas.

## Common Pitfalls
- **Crossing the Axes**: Drawing the hyperbola so that it intersects the x-axis or y-axis, failing to understand the domain ($x \neq 0$) and range ($y \neq 0$).
- **Monotonicity Across Branches**: Incorrectly stating that "the function is decreasing for all $x$" when $k>0$, instead of properly stating "decreasing for $x>0$ and decreasing for $x<0$". The function is not decreasing across the discontinuity at $x=0$.
- **Confusing with Linear Functions**: Mixing up the behaviors of $y = kx$ and $y = \frac{k}{x}$, especially how changes in $x$ affect $y$.

## Parent Guidance
- **Emphasize the Constant Product**: Help your child understand inverse functions by focusing on the core concept: $x \times y = \text{constant}$. For example, if a journey is 120 miles, driving at 60 mph takes 2 hours, and driving at 40 mph takes 3 hours ($60 \times 2 = 40 \times 3 = 120$).
- **Graphing Diligence**: Ensure they plot enough points (especially fractions and decimals) near the origin to correctly capture the sharp curve of the hyperbola before it flattens out along the asymptotes.
- **Area Visualization**: Draw a graph of $y = \frac{6}{x}$. Pick various points like $(2,3)$, $(3,2)$, $(6,1)$. Have your child draw the rectangles to the axes and calculate the area to visually prove the area is always $|k| = 6$.

## Linked Mistake Concepts
- **Fractions and Division by Zero**: A weak understanding of why division by zero is undefined makes it hard to grasp asymptotes.
- **Solving Fractional Equations**: Difficulties in manipulating and solving equations with variables in the denominator (learned in the fractions chapter) can hinder solving inverse proportional problems.
- **Direct vs. Inverse Proportion**: In word problems, students often mistakenly set up a direct proportion ratio ($y_1/x_1 = y_2/x_2$) instead of the correct inverse product ($x_1y_1 = x_2y_2$).

## Examples
**Example Problem:**
The graph of an inverse proportional function $y = \frac{k}{x}$ passes through the point $A(-2, 3)$. 
1. Find the value of $k$ and the expression for the function.
2. Does the point $B(1.5, -4)$ lie on this graph?
3. If points $C(x_1, y_1)$ and $D(x_2, y_2)$ are on the graph, and $x_1 < x_2 < 0$, compare the values of $y_1$ and $y_2$.

**Solution:**
```text
1. Since the graph passes through A(-2, 3), substitute x = -2 and y = 3 into the function:
3 = k / -2
k = 3 * (-2) = -6
The expression for the function is y = -6 / x.

2. To check if B(1.5, -4) is on the graph, substitute x = 1.5 into the function:
y = -6 / 1.5 = -4
Since the calculated y matches the y-coordinate of point B, point B(1.5, -4) lies on the graph. (Alternatively, check if x * y = k: 1.5 * -4 = -6).

3. The function is y = -6 / x. Since k = -6 < 0, the graph is located in the second and fourth quadrants.
In each quadrant, as x increases, y increases.
Since x_1 < x_2 < 0, both points C and D are in the second quadrant.
Because the function is increasing for x < 0, a larger x-value results in a larger y-value.
Therefore, since x_1 < x_2, it must be that y_1 < y_2.
```
